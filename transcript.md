Techn democracy is what I think may come after cryptocurrency. We go from
magical internet money to magical internet votes — and this is just an
example, yeah I'll show you a bunch of graphics in a second, a little "I
voted" tchotchke, and the running example will be my friend Brian Johnson
and how he might actually start a new party online and you get an "I voted"
sticker for him.

So let me give motivation for this concept — what is techn democracy, why do
we even want it — then let's visualize it, look at actually like a concrete
app example, and then how you might actually build this vision at the
Network School.

## What comes after cryptocurrency?

Right now if anybody's paying attention to the Western world or the world at
large, democratically elected leaders are very democratically unpopular.
This is from a few months ago, a few of these leaders have already basically
lost office. It really doesn't matter what side one is on — 49% is mad that
the other 51% is winning and vice versa. Global dissatisfaction of democracy
on all sides has actually just been ramping over the last several decades.

If we ask the question "what comes after cryptocurrency": the financial
crisis of 2008 led to cryptocurrency, and in my view the political crisis of
2024 will lead to techn democracy. To motivate this — Ron Paul and "End the
Fed" and that sentiment in the late 2000s and early 2010s led to Bitcoin,
and that wasn't abandoning capitalism, it was in a sense revitalizing it and
rebuilding it internet first. The declining confidence in our institutions
now means we can actually rebuild democracy internet first. We've done it
for capitalism with crypto, now I think we'll do it for democracy.

Let's say my friend collaborator colleague Brian Johnson founded the Don't

Here is an example app that could be built on Farcaster. A client he could

## What happens when you Apply?

Let's say you actually went and applied to the Don't Die Party. What would
happen in this hypothetical concept of techn democracy? It actually has
three parts:

1. **Follow.** You would follow not just the president of the party but the
   VP and the Treasurer. You'd essentially follow the party leaders.

2. **Fund.** You would fund the party — pay party dues. This just by itself
   makes it much more serious than typical political parties today because
   it actually makes demands on one's time and energy. In return for this
   funding you get (in this case) blueprint olive oil, membership NFT, and
   you also start to attend events. The funds are used for organizing party
   events.

3. **Franchise.** The most important and the most technically interesting:
   you actually vote for the slate of all of these candidates and you give
   them limited but real permissions over your crypto wallet. Just like
   OAuth or wallet permissions, you flip some sliders and you say:
   - "I'm voting for them for President, VP and Treasurer"
   - "I'm committing to follow norms and joining their Discord community"
   - "Committing $100 — and if I violate those norms, they can slash those
     $100, for example."

## Bitcoin started small

To be clear, this is kind of like Bitcoin started as just play internet
money. It didn't start at where it is today which is a trillion dollar
market cap. It basically started at 1 cent when it finally got a price. So
the first techn democratic elections of this kind are like running for dog
catcher — they're the most limited simple kinds of things you could
imagine. But you are giving this leader limited power over your crypto
wallet.

## The Franchise as primitive

This limited yet binding grant of permissions over your wallet to a digital
party is everything. From that single primitive you can rebuild so much.

You've actually done this already — when you give wallet permissions, you
are saying "OK here is my Ethereum account, here is a third party app, I'm
trusting Ethereum the blockchain to give limited permissions over my wallet
to this app for the greater good."

You also do this off-chain with something like OAuth — you give limited
permissions over your digital property to a third party app for the greater
good.

This is actually very structurally similar to voting for somebody for
office: you're saying "I want this person to have some power over me, some
ability to tax, some ability to regulate. I will give up some of my assets,
some of my control, but I think it'll be for the greater good — they'll be
able to allocate public goods for everybody's benefit."

It's actually more like joining a company and signing a contract than the
typical vote which is not binding on either side. A typical campaign promise
is not binding, but something like this is.

## The innovation

Delegating permissions not to a third-party app but to a new digital
party. From a third party app to a third party — or a fourth or a fifth.

You get your **vote receipt** — your NFT, your on-chain thing. It's a
**cryptographically verifiable vote** which you can prove you joined, you
can see who others joined, and any third party can verify that these are
real people rather than bots. **Proof by running, proof of human.** You can
view everybody on Etherscan who voted for your party and you can actually
prove to the world that this polity exists. Nobody can deny it.

This is borderless just like cryptocurrency. The old borders don't matter.
You can actually put together a party of like-mind and start rebuilding
polities.

When you join a party like this you attend party events — that's where the
budget is going. Something in Chicago, something in Amsterdam, something in
Bombay. You attend those events — in this case longevity meetups or
workouts.

## Why now

Everything else is online. In 1990 you were sending physical mail, using
cash. Today 99% of transactions and communications are online. But
elections and legislation aren't yet fully online — though they are
partially online.

How institutional voting works in Western democracies has three parts:
1. **The Twitter vote** — millions of followers
2. **The fundraising vote** — millions of dollars
3. **The Fiat off-chain vote** — the actual vote

These are different races that correlate but aren't exactly the same.

Online voting also exists in three kinds:
1. Daily Reddit/Twitter/Hacker News upvotes that determine what goes viral
2. DAO votes that allocate millions of dollars out of treasuries
3. Estonia actually does internet voting using hardware wallet equivalents
   for cryptographically secure online voting

The technology and precedent are there. A lot of leader selection happens
online but it's kind of in the PayPal era for Bitcoin — we haven't taken
that final leap of putting the settlement layer on chain. **The same
technology that securely sends one coin can securely log one vote.** We
move the settlement layer on chain, from Fiat to crypto.

## This is binding, not LARP

The digital vote I'm talking about is binding. It's not a LARP. The reason
is **crypto wallets control real resources.** If you have a million crypto
wallets whose partial control is pledged to a political leader with proof
of consent and time limitations, you've got real resources.

There are four phases of digital governance:
1. **Discords** — transactions, messaging access via crypto wallet
2. **DAOs** — collective voting power, allocate millions of dollars
3. **Doors** — NFT smart locks, your Ethereum wallet unlocks a physical
   door. The same prompt of "approve this transaction" turns a physical
   lock. That's the door to a hotel room — but could be the door to your
   car, a crane, a plane, a truck, any piece of capital equipment.
4. **Drones** — the keys to humanoid robots, self-driving cars, drones.
   This can script the physical world. That's where we're going over the
   next 5–10 years.

Most Web2 systems get hacked but Web3 systems don't. The control plane for
drones, self-driving cars, anything really important will eventually go
on-chain. Your private keys are anything important to you.

If a million people grant a wallet permission and only under certain
conditions can someone search their premises, you can take millions of
people's crypto assets and start aggregating them into a digital polity.
**Crypto wallets can control real resources. It's not a LARP.** It'll be
thought of as a LARP for a long time, just like Bitcoin was a LARP for a
long time. Again you'll start with elections for dog catcher but it can
really reach unlimited levels of resources over time.

## Key features of techn democracy

- **Cryptographically verifiable vote.** Every vote counts because you can
  count all the votes. Same technology used to verify blockchains. As
  people have less trust in elections, we restore it.
- **Streaming vote.** Not "election day." When someone wants to pledge
  support to a candidate for a year, two years, five years, they just do
  so. It's a streaming vote — they start accumulating support.
- **Social smart contract.** A portmanteau of Rousseau, Nick Szabo, and
  Vitalik. The rights of the governed are clearly enumerated. You're not
  opting in to something you didn't consent to.
- **Provably limited government.** A maximum tax rate. When you opt into
  this polity it can only take, in this case, $100 out of your wallet. It
  can't take $1,000. All these permissions we've built for crypto things,
  we now use for governance.
- **Truly universal franchise.** Anybody from anywhere can vote for anyone.
  Just like crypto — anybody can send money to anyone, anybody can pledge
  to anyone. Bust all the old legacy borders.
- **Truly universal candidacy.** In the US you have to be 35 years old and
  a natural-born citizen to be president. So Elon couldn't be president.
  Many immigrant founders couldn't. Only ~2% of the world satisfies that.
  But 100% of the world could become president of a network state. They
  could hold a techn democratic election, build a party, build legitimacy
  online, show that millions of people voted for them — and build a new
  country in the cloud, then get land on Earth.
- **Truly new polities.** Throw out legacy denominators. The denominator is
  everybody who's opted in. Non-geographical, reflects people's consent.
- **Digital delegation.** You never have to ask "who's the president, who's
  in charge, what powers does this bureaucrat have." Permission grants
  like Google Docs — grant edit access, view access, audit, revoke.
- **100% democracy.** Not 51%. The whole point of democracy is consent of
  the governed. Now we get 100% consent like a company — nobody is part of
  this party unless they want to be. Double opt in every day.
- **0.1% democracy.** A tiny group of people can form a new political
  party. 0.1% of the world is still 8 million people.

## Three-step Apply (the canonical UX)

The applicant's flow when joining a party:

```
1. Follow      → follow President + VP + Treasurer (slate)
2. Fund        → pay annual party dues; receive membership NFT
3. Franchise   → grant scoped wallet permissions:
                 - Vote for the slate (President, VP, Treasurer)
                 - Commit to follow norms; pay annual dues
                 - Allow slashing up to $X for norm violations
                 - Name the President as dispute resolver
```

Each is a toggle in the consent UI — like Google OAuth, but for parties.
